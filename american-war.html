War against the American

1954 - 1975

Vietnam Resistance Against American Intervention

An overview of the Vietnam War and its key events

Following the division of Vietnam in 1954, the struggle for reunification became inevitable. The involvement of the United States led to a new and prolonged war. From the formation of the National Liberation Front to the Tet Offensive, the war against the United States was marked by intense conflict and political maneuvers, ultimately resulting in Vietnam's reunification in 1975.

"We will fight until there is not a single American soldier left in Vietnam."
- Le Duan

Tet Mau Than Offensive

The Tet Offensive of 1968 was a major military campaign during the Vietnam War, launched by North Vietnamese and Viet Cong forces against South Vietnam and its allies, including the United States. Beginning on January 30, 1968, during the Lunar New Year (Tet) holiday, it consisted of coordinated attacks on over 100 cities and military bases. Although it was a tactical defeat for the North due to heavy casualties, the offensive shocked the United States and significantly weakened public support for the war, marking a turning point in the conflict.

"The Tet Offensive showed the American people that the war in Vietnam was far from over."
- General Vo Nguyen Giap

Paris Accords

The Paris Peace Accords, signed on January 27, 1973, aimed to establish peace and end the Vietnam War. The agreement involved the United States, North Vietnam, South Vietnam, and the Viet Cong, calling for a ceasefire, the withdrawal of U.S. troops, and the release of prisoners of war. While it temporarily reduced direct U.S. involvement, the accords failed to resolve underlying tensions, leading to the eventual fall of Saigon and the reunification of Vietnam under communist control in 1975.

"The Accords marked a turning point, but they did not end the war."
- Former U.S. Secretary of State Henry Kissinger

Fall of Saigon

The Fall of Saigon on April 30, 1975, marked the end of the Vietnam War as North Vietnamese forces captured Saigon, the capital of South Vietnam. The event followed the successful Ho Chi Minh Campaign, which overwhelmed South Vietnamese defenses. Saigon’s fall led to the collapse of the South Vietnamese government and the reunification of Vietnam under communist rule. It also symbolized a significant defeat for the United States, which had supported South Vietnam throughout the war. Images of helicopters evacuating U.S. personnel and refugees became iconic representations of the war's end.

"Sài Gòn đã hoàn toàn giải phóng." (Saigon has been completely liberated.)
- North Vietnamese Forces

Legacy of the War against the American

The war against the United States, which spanned two decades, had a profound impact on Vietnam and the world. It led to the reunification of Vietnam under communist rule, ended U.S. military involvement in Southeast Asia, and marked a significant chapter in the global anti-imperialist struggle. While the war caused immense suffering and loss, it also demonstrated the resilience of the Vietnamese people and their determination to regain their independence. The legacy of the war is remembered both in Vietnam and abroad, shaping the future of both nations.
