War against the French

1945 - 1954

Vietnam Struggle Against French Colonial Rule

An overview of key events from the First Indochina War

The First Indochina War or the War Against the French, was a significant period in Vietnamese history. Beginning in 1945, the Vietnamese fought to end decades of French colonial rule. This struggle culminated in a resounding victory at Dien Bien Phu in 1954, which signaled the end of French colonialism in Indochina. Below is an overview of the key events that marked this pivotal struggle.

Battle of Hanoi

The Battle of Hanoi (December 19, 1946 – February 17, 1947) marked the beginning of the First Indochina War. The Viet Minh launched a coordinated uprising against French forces to resist colonial rule. Using guerrilla tactics and defensive trenches, they attacked key French positions across Hanoi. Despite their eventual withdrawal after intense fighting, the battle symbolized Vietnamese determination and marked the transition to a prolonged war for independence. Though the French retained control of Hanoi, the battle demonstrated the resilience of the Viet Minh, laying the groundwork for their ultimate victory at Dien Bien Phu in 1954.

"We would rather sacrifice everything than lose our country and not be slaves."
- President Ho Chi Minh

Border Campaign

The Border Campaign of 1950 was a significant Viet Minh offensive aimed at securing Vietnam’s northern border with China. By capturing key French outposts such as Cao Bang and Lang Son, the Viet Minh opened crucial supply routes for weapons and resources from China. Led by General Vo Nguyen Giap, the campaign marked the Viet Minh’s first large-scale victory, showcasing their growing strength and boosting morale for the struggle against French forces.

"On September 9, 1950, President Ho Chi Minh issued an appeal to the people of three provinces of Cao - Bac - Lang: 'Compatriots, please step forward to be a model for the people of the whole country, helping the Campaign to win.'"
- President Ho Chi Minh

Battle of Dien Bien Phu

The Battle of Dien Bien Phu (March 13 – May 7, 1954) was the decisive engagement that ended the First Indochina War. The Viet Minh, led by General Vo Nguyen Giap, encircled and besieged French forces in a remote valley. Using innovative strategies, they overcame the French’s superior firepower. After 56 days of intense fighting, the French garrison surrendered, marking a catastrophic defeat for France and leading to the Geneva Accords, which ended French colonial rule in Vietnam.

"The victory at Dien Bien Phu was not only a military victory but also a victory of the will and spirit of the people."
- General Vo Nguyen Giap

Geneva Accords

The Geneva Accords, signed in July 1954, were a set of agreements that ended the First Indochina War and marked the withdrawal of French forces from Vietnam. The accords temporarily divided Vietnam at the 17th parallel, with the North under the Viet Minh and the South under a pro-Western regime. It also called for national elections in 1956 to unify the country, though these elections were never held. While the accords sought peace, they ultimately set the stage for the Vietnam War as tensions between North and South escalated into a full-scale conflict.

Legacy of the War Against the French

The victory over French colonial rule in 1954 was a monumental achievement in the history of Vietnam. It marked the end of over 80 years of French domination and set the stage for the eventual reunification of Vietnam. This victory also played a crucial role in the broader anti-colonial struggle worldwide, inspiring many other nations to fight for their independence from colonial powers.
